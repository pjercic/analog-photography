# 🔍 Physics: Lenses, Focal Length, and the Sliding Box Camera

> **Level:** 2 (Secondary School)

## 🌟 The Big Question
In our Level 1 experiment with the Camera Obscura, we discovered a problem: if we make the pinhole tiny, the picture is sharp but too dark to see. If we make the hole big, it is bright enough, but a blurry mess! 

How can we have a giant hole to let in lots of light, but still keep the picture perfectly sharp? The answer is **glass**.

---

## 1. The Physics of Refraction
Light loves to travel in straight lines, and it travels fastest in a vacuum (space). When light hits a denser material, like water or glass, it slows down. 

When light hits a piece of glass at an angle, this change in speed causes the light ray to **bend**. This bending of light is called **Refraction**. 


By shaping the glass perfectly, we can control exactly *where* the light bends.

---

## 2. The Convex Lens & Focal Length
To fix our dark Camera Obscura, we need a **Convex Lens**. This is a piece of glass that is thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges (like a magnifying glass).

Here is how it solves our problem:
1. We make a giant hole in our camera to let in millions of light rays.
2. We place a convex lens over the hole.
3. The lens takes all those messy, spread-out light rays and **refracts** them, bending them all back together to meet at one single, sharp point.



### What is Focal Length?
If you point a convex lens at the Sun (or anything very far away), the light rays will bend and meet at a specific point behind the lens. The distance from the center of the lens to that sharp meeting point is called the **Focal Length** ($f$). 

* A "fat" lens bends light sharply. It has a **short focal length** (e.g., 28mm). It gives a wide-angle view.
* A "flat" lens bends light gently. It has a **long focal length** (e.g., 200mm). It acts like a telescope.

---

## 3. The Math of Focusing
There is one big catch to using a lens. A pinhole camera is *always* in focus, no matter how far away the wall is. A lens is very picky. 

To understand why camera lenses have to twist and move in and out, we use the **Lens Formula**:

$$\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}$$

* **$f$** = Focal Length (The power of your lens, this never changes).
* **$d_o$** = Distance to the Object (How far away your friend is from the camera).
* **$d_i$** = Distance to the Image (Where the sharp picture forms inside the camera).

### The Focusing Rule
Because $f$ stays the same, if your friend walks closer to the camera ($d_o$ gets smaller), the sharp picture inside the camera moves further back ($d_i$ gets bigger)! 

If the film in your camera cannot move, the picture will become blurry. **To focus a camera, you are physically moving the lens closer to or further from the film to catch the moving focal point.**

---

## 4. The Build: The Sliding Box Camera
To put this math to the test, we are going to build a camera that can actually focus. 

Instead of one cardboard box, we will build *two* boxes that slide inside of one another. The front box will hold a magnifying glass (the lens), and the back box will hold a piece of tracing paper (the screen). By sliding the boxes back and forth, you can change the distance ($d_i$) and focus on objects both near and far!
