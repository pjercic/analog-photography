# 02 Chemistry Sun

# ☀️ Chemistry: Sun Prints & The Power of Invisible Light

> **Level:** 1 (Primary School)
> **Topic:** Photochemistry & UV Light
> **Prerequisite:** None

## 🌟 The Big Question
Can light change what an object is made of?

If you leave a bright plastic toy in the yard all summer, what happens? It fades! The color disappears. The Sun isn't just a giant flashlight; it is a massive chemical engine. In this lesson, we will learn how the Sun's energy can actually create and destroy colors.

---

## 1. The Science of Invisible Light (UV)
The light we can see (red, yellow, green, blue) is only a small part of what the Sun sends to Earth. The Sun also sends out **Invisible Light**. 

One type of invisible light is called **Ultraviolet (UV) light**. UV light carries a lot of energy. When UV light hits certain materials, its energy is so strong that it forces the tiny building blocks of that material (molecules) to rearrange themselves. 

* When UV light hits your skin, it causes a chemical reaction that makes a sunburn or a tan!
* When UV light hits a photograph, we call it **Photochemistry**. 

---

## 2. The Magic Trick: Cyanotypes
A **Cyanotype** is one of the oldest types of photography in the world. "Cyan" means blue. 

Instead of a camera, we use a special piece of paper coated with iron-based chemicals. 
1.  **In the dark:** The chemicals are pale green and resting.
2.  **In the Sun:** The UV light hits the chemicals and gives them energy. They react and change into a deep, permanent blue color.
3.  **In the water:** We wash the paper. The water acts as a "lock." It washes away any green chemicals that didn't get hit by the Sun, leaving only the blue behind.

If we put an object (like a leaf or a toy) on top of the paper, the object's **shadow** blocks the UV rays. The paper underneath the object stays green, and washes away to become pure white!

## 3. Hands-On Activity: Making a Sun Print

> **Teacher Note:** For primary students, use pre-coated "Sunprint" or "Nature Print" paper. Mixing raw Cyanotype chemicals (Potassium Ferricyanide) should be reserved for High School levels.

### 🧰 Materials Needed:
* Pre-coated Sunprint Paper (keep it hidden in a black bag until you are ready!)
* A piece of clear glass or heavy plastic (acrylic)
* A tub of plain tap water
* Interesting objects: Leaves, flowers, keys, lace, or cut-out paper shapes.

### 🏗️ Step-by-Step Instructions:
1. **The Setup (Indoors):** Keep away from windows! Pull out one sheet of Sunprint paper and place it blue-side up on a piece of cardboard.
2. **The Composition:** Arrange your leaves and toys on top of the paper. Place the clear glass on top to squish everything down flat.
3. **The Exposure:** Carry the cardboard outside into direct sunlight. Watch the paper! Over 2 to 5 minutes, you will see the blue paper turn very pale blue or white. This means the chemical reaction is happening!
4. **The Wash:** Bring the paper back inside. Take the objects off. Quickly dunk the paper into the tub of water for 1 minute. 
5. **The Magic Reversal:** Watch as the colors flip! The bright parts turn dark blue, and the shadows turn stark white. Lay it flat to dry.

---

## 4. The Art Connection: Positive and Negative Space
When we paint a picture of a tree, we are painting the **Positive Space** (the tree itself). 

But in a Sun Print, we are doing the exact opposite. We are coloring the empty air *around* the tree, and leaving the tree totally blank. Artists call this empty area the **Negative Space**. 

### 📝 Discussion Questions
1. Why didn't the paper turn blue under the glass? *(Hint: Does regular window glass block UV light?)*
2. If you left the paper in the sun for only 10 seconds, what would happen? What if you left it for an hour?
3. How is a Cyanotype similar to the Camera Obscura we built in the last lesson? How is it different?
